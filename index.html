Structured Summary of the Thesis: "Estimation spatialisée de la biomasse et des besoins en eau des cultures à l'aide de données satellitales à hautes résolutions spatiale et temporelle"

Author: Martin Claverie
Institution: Université Paul Sabatier – Toulouse III
Field: Télédétection de la Biosphère Continentale – Modélisation

Classification under Thematic Areas

Thematic Area: Earth Observation and Geo-Information
This thesis falls under Earth observation and geo-information as it utilizes satellite data for agricultural monitoring, specifically biomass estimation and water requirement assessment. The study integrates remote sensing, biophysical modeling, and in situ measurements to analyze agroecosystems in southwest France.

1. Abstract

This research aims to develop methods for quantifying two key variables in agriculture: crop biomass (linked to the carbon cycle) and water needs (linked to the hydrological cycle) over large areas. The approach integrates:

  1. Crop models (SAFY and SAFYE).
  2. Satellite data (high-resolution Formosat-2 imagery).
  3. In situ measurements (biophysical variables such as Green Area Index - GAI).

The study compares two GAI estimation methods:

  • Empirical method: Using NDVI-based relationships.
  • Physical method: Radiative transfer model inversion (PROSAIL).

Results show that both methods yield similar accuracy (~31% error), with the physical approach proving robust and reducing reliance on ground measurements. SAFY estimates biomass with a 29% error, while SAFYE estimates evapotranspiration with a 35% error. The models reproduce annual yield trends for sunflower and soybean but not for maize. Water consumption estimates align with observed interannual variations.

 

2. Summary

Research Problem

Climate change threatens water availability, impacting agricultural production. The 2007 IPCC report highlighted agriculture’s role in carbon sequestration and the vulnerability of crop yields to water scarcity. This study develops a satellite-driven approach to estimate biomass and water requirements at regional scales.

Methodology

  • Study Area: Southwest France (non-irrigated sunflower, irrigated maize & soybean).
  • Satellite Data: Formosat-2 (8m spatial resolution, daily temporal resolution).
  • Ground Measurements: Biophysical variable measurements (GAI, biomass, evapotranspiration).
  • Models Used:
    • SAFY (Simple Algorithm for Yield Estimates): Biomass estimation.
    • SAFYE (Simple Algorithm for Yield and Evapotranspiration Estimates): Biomass and water balance.
  • GAI Estimation Methods:
    • NDVI-based empirical model.
    • Radiative transfer model (PROSAIL) inversion.
  • Validation: Experimental data from 2006-2010.

Key Findings

  • GAI estimation: Physical and empirical methods provide similar accuracy (31% error).
  • Biomass estimation: SAFY achieves a 29% relative error.
  • Evapotranspiration modeling: SAFYE underestimates evapotranspiration (35% error).
  • Crop yield prediction:
    • Sunflower & soybean: Good annual yield trend reproduction.
    • Maize: Poor agreement.
  • Water consumption: SAFYE effectively models interannual water usage variation.

Structured Summary of Claverie et al. (2013)

Title: Validation of coarse spatial resolution LAI and FAPAR time series over cropland in southwest France
Source: Remote Sensing of Environment, Volume 139, Pages 216–230

1. Thematic Classification

Earth Observation and Geo-Information:

  • Utilizes satellite-derived Leaf Area Index (LAI) and Fraction of Absorbed Photosynthetically Active Radiation (FAPAR) time series.
  • Focuses on remote sensing applications for agricultural monitoring, environmental modeling, and climate studies.
  • Assesses Essential Climate Variables (ECVs) using MODIS, Formosat-2, and radiative transfer models.

2. Abstract

This study validates LAI and FAPAR products derived from MODIS surface reflectance (MOD09CMG) at a coarse spatial resolution (0.05°) over cropland in southwest France. The validation is performed using high-resolution reference maps generated from Formosat-2 (8 m) data and in situ ground measurements collected between 2006 and 2010. The analysis evaluates the uncertainty of MODIS-derived products (MOD15A3, GEOv1) and compares them with new estimates derived from artificial neural networks (BV-NNET) using PROSAIL radiative transfer model simulations. Findings indicate that MODIS BV-NNET products achieve the best agreement with reference maps, with R² values exceeding 0.9 and relative uncertainties below 17%. The study also highlights the impact of bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) correction and compositing strategies on the accuracy and temporal consistency of LAI and FAPAR estimates.

3. Summary

Research Problem

  • Accurate estimation of LAI and FAPAR is critical for climate monitoring and agricultural applications.
  • Coarse-resolution products (MODIS, GEOv1) need validation against high-resolution satellite data and ground truth measurements.
  • Systematic errors in retrieval algorithms, BRDF correction, and temporal compositing require assessment.

Methodology

  • Satellite Data:
    • MODIS (MOD09CMG, MOD15A3) at 0.05° resolution.
    • Formosat-2 (8 m) for high-resolution reference maps.
  • Radiative Transfer Model:
    • PROSAIL used to simulate surface reflectances and derive ECV estimates.
  • Validation Strategy:
    • BV-NNET trained on PROSAIL simulations to derive MODIS LAI and FAPAR.
    • Formosat-2 reference maps validated against ground measurements from maize, sunflower, and soybean fields.
    • Comparison of MODIS-derived ECVs (MOD15A3, GEOv1, and BV-NNET) against reference data.
  • Metrics Used:
    • Accuracy, precision, uncertainty (root mean square deviation - RMSD), and correlation (R²).
    • Temporal noise assessment to evaluate smoothness of time series data.

Main Findings

  • Validation of Reference Maps:
    • LAI and FAPAR estimates from Formosat-2 were validated against ground measurements with uncertainties of 0.35 and 0.07, respectively.
  • MODIS BV-NNET Performance:
    • Achieved highest agreement with Formosat-2 reference maps (R² > 0.9, relative uncertainty <17%).
    • Use of BRDF-corrected MODIS reflectance improved LAI estimation but had limited effect on FAPAR uncertainty.
  • Product Comparisons:
    • MOD15A3 (MODIS official product): Underestimated LAI values compared to reference maps.
    • GEOv1 (European GEOLAND-2 product): Showed a systemic positive bias in both LAI and FAPAR.
    • BV-NNET MODIS Products: Showed best agreement with reference maps, particularly with BRDF correction.
  • Temporal Consistency:
    • Daily BV-NNET MODIS products exhibited better temporal stability compared to MOD15A3 (4-day composite).
    • GEOv1 (10-day composite) produced smooth time series but introduced systematic bias.
  • Resolution Effects:
    • Decreasing spatial resolution from 500 m to 4 km reduced uncertainty in LAI and FAPAR estimates.

Title: Maize and sunflower biomass estimation in southwest France using high spatial and temporal resolution remote sensing data
Authors: Martin Claverie et al.

2. Abstract

Objective & Research Question

The study explores the use of high spatial and temporal resolution remote sensing data (Formosat-2) to estimate crop biomass. It aims to couple Formosat-2 observations with a simple crop growth model (SAFY) to estimate maize and sunflower green area index (GAI) and dry aboveground biomass (DAM).

Key Findings & Conclusions

  • The SAFY model successfully estimated GAI and DAM, with a relative error of 28%.
  • Maize and sunflower growth patterns were distinguished using light-use efficiency (LUE) values, revealing stress effects due to water availability.
  • The estimated grain yields correlated well with French Agricultural Statistics, although discrepancies were noted for maize.
  • The study confirms the suitability of high-resolution optical remote sensing (e.g., Venμs, Sentinel-2) for large-scale agricultural monitoring.

Scientific & Practical Relevance

This work demonstrates the effectiveness of high-resolution satellite data in agricultural biomass estimation and has applications in precision farming, carbon cycle modeling, and food security assessment.

3. Summary

Research Problem

Agriculture covers 35% of the Earth's surface and acts as a carbon sink. However, crop production is highly variable due to climate, soil properties, and agricultural practices. Remote sensing offers a solution, but previous satellites lacked both high spatial and temporal resolution needed for accurate agricultural monitoring. The Formosat-2 satellite addresses this limitation, offering daily 8m-resolution imagery.

Methodology

Study Area

  • 24 × 24 km² area near Toulouse, France
  • Maize (irrigated) and sunflower (non-irrigated) were the target crops
  • Meteorological data from SAFRAN (temperature, radiation, precipitation)

Data Collection & Processing

  • 95 Formosat-2 images (2006-2009)
  • Field measurements: Green Area Index (GAI), Dry Aboveground Biomass (DAM)
  • GAI estimation from NDVI:
    • GAI=k1×ek2×NDVI−k3GAI = k_1 \times e^{k_2 \times NDVI - k_3}
    • Calibration using hemispherical photographs

SAFY Model Integration

  • Daily simulation of leaf growth, biomass, and photosynthesis
  • Six key parameters calibrated using satellite data
  • Distinction between C3 (sunflower) and C4 (maize) photosynthetic pathways
  • Validation against in-situ biomass data and regional crop yield statistics

Main Findings & Conclusions

  1. GAI and DAM were successfully estimated: The SAFY model reproduced diverse growth patterns across different years and conditions.
  2. Light-use efficiency (LUE) varied by crop type and stress factors:
    • Maize LUE was affected mainly by temperature stress (since it was irrigated).
    • Sunflower LUE was influenced by water stress (due to rainfall dependence).
  3. Biomass estimation accuracy was 72% (relative error: 28%):
    • Maize biomass was slightly underestimated, while sunflower was overestimated due to simplifications in senescence modeling.
  4. Grain yield estimates agreed with French Agricultural Statistics for Haute-Garonne, but maize showed larger discrepancies due to model limitations.
  5. Future missions (Venμs, Sentinel-2) will enhance crop monitoring by providing continuous high-resolution observations.

Using Thermal Time and Pixel Purity for Enhancing Biophysical Variable Time Series: An Interproduct Comparison
Authors: Grégory Duveiller, Frédéric Baret, Pierre Defourny
Published in IEEE Transactions on Geoscience and Remote Sensing, Vol. 51, No. 4, April 2013

Classification: Earth Observation and Geo-Information

The study falls under Earth observation and geo-information, focusing on enhancing biophysical variable time series using satellite-derived data. It contributes to vegetation monitoring, particularly for agricultural applications.

1. Abstract

This study evaluates and compares different global leaf area index (LAI) products using satellite imagery to improve vegetation monitoring. It compares the 1-km MODIS (MCD15) and CYCLOPES LAI products with a crop-specific green area index (GAI) retrieved from 250-m MODIS imagery. The enhanced GAI incorporates three processing techniques:

  1. Pixel purity filtering to improve crop-specific accuracy.
  2. Thermal time transformation to standardize temporal variations.
  3. A canopy structural dynamic model (CSDM) to smooth time series.

Results indicate that the 250-m GAI provides a more detailed and realistic crop-specific time series. However, applying thermal time and pixel purity techniques to 1-km products also improves their temporal consistency. The study suggests that these techniques could help harmonize satellite data for better vegetation monitoring.

2. Summary of the Study

Research Problem

Accurately monitoring vegetation growth using satellite-based LAI products is challenging due to differences in spatial and temporal resolution. Standard global LAI datasets often lack precision for specific crops, such as winter wheat, due to coarse resolution and inconsistencies in temporal data.

Methodologies

  • Study Area: Walloon region, Belgium (2003–2007), focusing on winter wheat fields.
  • Data Sources: MODIS (MCD15) and CYCLOPES LAI (1-km resolution), plus a crop-specific GAI (250-m MODIS imagery).
  • Processing Techniques Applied:
    • Pixel Purity Filtering: Only data from pure winter wheat pixels (≥75% purity) were used for the 250-m GAI.
    • Thermal Time Transformation: Time was converted to growing degree days (GDD) to account for temperature-driven plant growth variability.
    • Canopy Structural Dynamic Model (CSDM): A model was fitted to smooth the LAI/GAI time series, reducing noise and improving consistency.

Key Findings

  • The 250-m GAI dataset provided a more realistic seasonal growth pattern for winter wheat.
  • Applying thermal time transformation improved temporal smoothness in all datasets.
  • The CSDM method reduced noise and enhanced consistency in both 250-m and 1-km datasets.
  • The MODIS and CYCLOPES LAI products underestimated peak GAI values and showed higher variability.
  • Applying pixel purity and thermal time techniques to the 1-km LAI products significantly improved their accuracy.

Harmonized Landsat Sentinel-2 (HLS)

Structured Summary of the Scientific Paper: "The Harmonized Landsat and Sentinel-2 Surface Reflectance Data Set"

Thematic Area: Earth Observation and Geo-Information

This paper falls under the category of Earth observation and geo-information, specifically focusing on satellite-based data harmonization, remote sensing applications for land monitoring, and advancements in analysis-ready datasets.

1. Abstract

The study presents the Harmonized Landsat and Sentinel-2 (HLS) project, a NASA initiative aimed at generating a Virtual Constellation (VC) using surface reflectance (SR) data from Landsat 8's Operational Land Imager (OLI) and Sentinel-2's Multi-Spectral Instrument (MSI). The HLS dataset is designed for seamless integration of these sensors by employing a suite of radiometric and geometric harmonization techniques. The study outlines key methodologies, including atmospheric correction, cloud masking, spatial co-registration, bi-directional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) normalization, and spectral bandpass adjustment. The dataset includes three primary products:

  1. S10 - Full-resolution Sentinel-2 MSI SR (10 m, 20 m, 60 m)
  2. S30 - 30 m Sentinel-2 MSI Nadir BRDF-Adjusted Reflectance (NBAR)
  3. L30 - 30 m Landsat 8 OLI NBAR

The research highlights the advantages of HLS in enhancing temporal resolution for Earth observation applications, including land cover monitoring, agricultural management, disaster response, and vegetation phenology. However, limitations remain, particularly in Sentinel-2 cloud detection accuracy. Cross-validation with MODIS data shows that HLS maintains a high degree of consistency, with overall deviations below 11%.

2. Summary

2.1 Research Problem

Satellite-based land monitoring requires frequent, high-resolution observations, which individual sensors cannot provide due to their revisit cycles (e.g., Landsat 8: 16-day, Sentinel-2A/B: 5-day). The challenge is to harmonize these independent datasets into a single, analysis-ready product for improved monitoring of land surface changes, vegetation dynamics, water resources, and disaster impacts.

2.2 Methodology

The HLS processing chain comprises the following key steps:

  1. Atmospheric Correction:
    • Utilizes the Land Surface Reflectance Code (LaSRC), an algorithm based on MODIS MCD09 and LEDAPS techniques.
    • Corrects for molecular scattering, water vapor, ozone absorption, and aerosols using MODIS and NCEP GDAS inputs.
  2. Cloud and Shadow Masking:
    • Landsat 8: Uses LaSRC’s cloud mask, which integrates thermal band thresholding (305 K) and AOT inversion residuals.
    • Sentinel-2: Uses an adapted version of Fmask (Zhu et al., 2015), but performance is limited due to the lack of thermal data.
  3. Spatial Co-Registration and Gridding:
    • Addresses misalignment issues between Landsat 8 and Sentinel-2 by applying the Automated Registration and Orthorectification Package (AROP).
    • Reduces geolocation errors to 18.8 m (Landsat) and 6.2 m (Sentinel-2).
  4. BRDF Normalization:
    • Implements the c-factor technique with MODIS-derived coefficients (Roy et al., 2016) to normalize angular differences in reflectance values.
  5. Bandpass Adjustment:
    • Corrects spectral differences between OLI and MSI using a regression model derived from Hyperion hyperspectral data.

2.3 Main Findings and Conclusions

  • HLS successfully harmonizes Landsat 8 and Sentinel-2 datasets, producing a near-daily, 30-meter resolution dataset.
  • Cross-validation with MODIS confirms high accuracy, with deviations not exceeding 11%, except for the blue band (~30% bias).
  • Cloud masking remains a challenge, particularly for Sentinel-2, leading to potential omissions.
  • Geometric accuracy is significantly improved, reducing misregistration errors.
  • The harmonized dataset provides a reliable time series for monitoring land surface changes and has been adopted for phenology, agricultural monitoring, and climate research.
